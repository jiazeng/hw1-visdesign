INFO 474: Visualization Design

Scatter Plot: The Effectiveness of Antibiotics

What is the general effectiveness of antibiotics (Penicillin, Streptomycin, Neomycin) for different bacterial infection?

The first visualization is a line chart that describes the bacteria’s minimum inhibitory concentration (MIC) of three antibiotics (penicillin, streptomycin, neomycin). This visualization answers the question that what is the general effectiveness of antibiotics for different bacterial infection. This line graph uses mainly two encodings: position, and color hue. Each encoding is very useful in showing the point of the chart.

Position on the x-axis allows the corresponding bacteria name. Position on the y-axis shows the antibiotics’ MIC number for the bacteria. To better display the data, I changed the y-axis into logarithmic scale because there is a large range of quantities and it is hard to show the differences in data with a linear scale. Color encoding used in the chart helps the reader identify the 3 types of antibiotics by differentiate the color. The color blue corresponds to Penicillin, orange corresponds to streptomycin, and green corresponds to neomycin.

During my design decision, I considered using both a line chart and a scatter plot. The reason that I chose to use a scatter plot rather than a line chart is because it only shows the data that has actual meaning. If I use a line chart, the data in between the bacteria will have no meaning and may be misleading. I also want to reveal if there is a relationship of the effectiveness of the antibiotics toward the same bacterial infection. For example, the chart shows that all three antibiotics are less effective for bacteria such as Mycobacterium tuberculosis, while they are more effective for Brucella Anthracis. The chart also shows that Streptomycin and Neomycin have relatively similar performance in comparison with Penicillin.

Grouped Bar Chart: The Comparison of the Antibiotics Performance

Which antibiotics work best with each bacterial infection?

The second chart is a grouped bar chart that uses 3 different encodings: color, position, and length/area. I chose to display the data with grouped bar chart because it is visually easy for viewers to compare among the three antibiotics category. Therefore, it is easy to answer the question of which one works best with each bacterial infection.

Color encoding is used to differentiate the three antibiotics. Each color corresponds to one antibiotic. It is very easy to identify by looking at the legend (key) on the right. Position on the x-axis shows the bacteria that the antibiotics are used for. Position on the y-axis shows the antibiotics’ MIC number for the bacteria. Viewers can compare the group by judging relative position along the y-axis. By comparing the position of the bars with the axis, people can tell the relative quantity that the bars represent. To better display the data, I changed the y-axis into logarithmic scale like the scatter plot because there is a large range of quantities and it is hard to show the differences in data with a linear scale. Moreover, this bar chart also uses length and area of the rectangles for people to decode the relative values that the bars represent in comparison to each other on the common scale.

In addition to the visual encodings, I also added labels and keys to help viewers better interpret the data. For example, when user hovers over the bars, it shows the detailed information of that data including the MIC of each antibiotic and the corresponding bacteria.
